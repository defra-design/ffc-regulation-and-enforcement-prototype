Salmonella testing: legal requirements

You must:

  • test for salmonella if you are a poultry keeper

  • take samples to test from chicks, pullets and adult hens in the specified way and send them to an approved UK laboratory

  • inform vet about a positive result (if testing laboratory doesnâ€™t do this automatically)

  • keep test, movement and medicine records for at least 2 years

  • tell APHA or DAERA when you expect 250 or more hens to arrive on your holding

.

Who must test for salmonella

Poultry (hen, duck, geese or turkey) keeper with 350 or more laying hens of the same species at any time in 12 months.

When must you test for salmonella

You must take samples from:

  • chicks on the day they arrive from a hatchery

  • pullets (young hens or rearing hens) 2 weeks before you move them to the laying unit

  • adult hens every 15 weeks during the laying period - start when they are 22 to 26 weeks old

When your flock does not need tested for salmonella

You do not need to test if:

  • you produce eggs for private domestic use (they are not entering the market)

  • you have fewer than 350 laying hens and supply the consumer direct (such as through farm-gate sales or local retailers)

  • your flock is already under official salmonella restrictions after a positive test (in this situation the flock is not part of the NCP as you cannot sell your eggs)

Before you can test for salmonella

  1. Choose an Department for the Environment and Rural Affairs (Defra) approved laboratory in Great Britain or DAERA approved laboratories in Northern Ireland

  1. Ask your vet or testing laboratory for advice about sampling plans and training staff to take the samples

  1. Make use of training schemes like the Lion Training Passport

  1. Get the sampling equipment

  1. Work out how many flocks you have

  1. Register all premises where you keep flocks
