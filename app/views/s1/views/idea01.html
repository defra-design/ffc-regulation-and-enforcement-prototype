Who must test for salmonella

Egg producers who keep 350 or more laying hens at any time in 12 months.

When to test for salmonella

You must take samples from:

* chicks on the day they arrive from a hatchery

* pullets (young hens or rearing hens) 2 weeks before you move them to the laying unit

* adult hens every 15 weeks during the laying period - start when they are 22 to 26 weeks old

When your flock does not need tested for salmonella

You do not need to test if:

* you produce eggs for private domestic use (they are not entering the market)

* you have fewer than 350 laying hens and supply the consumer direct (such as through farm-gate sales or local retailers)

* your flock is already under official salmonella restrictions after a positive test (in this situation the flock is not part of the NCP as you cannot sell your eggs)

Why do you need to test

As part of the national control programme (NCP), you must test laying hens for salmonella.

Other countries in Europe operate similar schemes. If you’re importing hatching eggs or day old chicks from Europe, check they’ve completed salmonella testing.

What you must do

Before you can test for salmonella

If you are new to keeping hens for egg laying, you need to:

* ask your vet or testing laboratory for advice on sampling plans and training staff to take the samples correctly

* make use of other training initiatives such as the Lion Training Passport

* choose an approved laboratory in the UK

* work out how many flocks you have to sample

* get the sampling kit (ask your vet, laboratory, APHA or DAERA in Northern Ireland

what you need)

* register the premises where you keep your flocks before you test

* tell APHA or DAERA before a laying flock arrives at your holding

Take samples

You must collect samples, label and send to the laboratory for testing.

Collect samples from chicks on the day they arrive from the hatchery

You should test:

* the liners (chick papers) from boxes used to deliver the chicks - one box for every 500 chicks from each delivery (up to 10 boxes)

* any chicks that are dead on arrival (up to 60 from each hatchery)

Samples to collect from pullets 2 weeks before you move them to the laying unit

You should test:

* 2 pairs of boot swabs (fabric overshoes) for your floor-reared birds

* 60g of a composite faeces sample

Composite means the faeces are from many birds in the flock.

Samples to collect from your adult egg-laying hens every 15 weeks during the laying period

If officials collect routine samples during the laying period, you do not need to test again for 15 weeks.

You must start to take samples from adult egg-laying hens in the layer unit or house when they’re 22 to 26 weeks old.

For barn or free-range flocks, you should test:

* 2 pairs of boot swabs when not part of a multi-tier system

* 1 pair of boot swabs and one or more hand-held faecal swabs when part of a multi-tier system

For cage flocks, you should test:

* 2 composite faeces samples of 150g each for houses with scrapers or belt cleaners

* one or more fabric swabs for houses without scrapers or belt cleaners

[Find out how to take boot swab, composite and fabric swab samples](https://www.gov.uk.

Keep records

You must keep records for at least 2 years.

Test records

For each sample you must record the:

* date you took the sample

* flock identification (this is the flock’s house name or number, and the month and year the flock moved into the house)

* type(s) of sample (such as boot swabs or pooled faeces)

* age of the flock sampled (there may be more than one age if the sample is from a multi-age house)

* laboratory that tested the sample

* test result

Movement records

You need to record any movement of birds or eggs (except eggs for human consumption) to or from your premises. For each movement record the:

* date of movement - the month and year the flock moved on to or off your premises

* number of birds or eggs

* age of the birds

* house name or number

* address that the birds or eggs moved from (including the building name or number)

* address that the birds or eggs moved to (including the building name or number)

Medication records

You need to keep medication records for the flock. You must record the:

* medicines used

* dose rate

* start and end date of treatment

* route of administration

* end date of the withdrawal period

What happens if your samples test positive for salmonella

Restrictions to your flocks and premises may apply and if so you’ll need to take actions.

You’ll need to inform your vet of the positive result, if you have not made arrangements with the testing laboratory to do this automatically.

In England, Scotland and Wales, if you suspect the result may have been due to an error (for example contamination of the original sample), you or your vet should contact APHA. If APHA agree, they’ll collect official confirmatory samples.

If confirmatory testing is completed, APHA may ask you to cull 5 birds. They’ll take the carcases and store their organs when they collect confirmatory samples. They may use these to test for the presence of antibiotics if the confirmatory test is negative.

Positive tests for Salmonella Enteritidis or Typhimurium

If APHA or DAERA confirm a non-vaccine strain of Salmonella Enteritidis or Salmonella Typhimurium (including monophasic Salmonella Typhimurium) in your flock, you must not sell your eggs from the positive flock as Class A.

The flock will be put under a legal restriction which will prohibit the sale of their eggs as Class A eggs. If official confirmatory sampling on your flock does not confirm the initial positive result, then you can resume selling the flock’s eggs as Class A.

If you want to sell your eggs as Class B, you must mark them:

* with the official Class B mark (a circle with the letter B inside)

* using a spray or food dye to leave an easily visible coloured mark at least 5 millimetres (mm) in diameter

Before you can sell any eggs, local egg marketing inspectors or poultry unit officials will visit your premises to check you’re complying with these restrictions. They can advise further on the egg marking requirements.

If your samples test positive for a regulated salmonella

You may want to request more tests at your own cost if you think a positive test result in an adult laying flock is false. You must do this operator optional testing within 4 weeks of the APHA or Agri-Food and Biosciences Institute.(AFBI) laboratory reporting the positive sample. For more information about this type of retesting or to get these tests arranged contact:

* APHA’s Customer Service Centre: One Health Worcester

* DAERA

APHA or DAERA will arrange for an official to supervise the collection and testing of your choice from the following sample types:

* 300 hen carcasses

* 4,000 eggs

* appropriate environmental sampling to suit your flock’s management ( for example cage, free-range)

The official may also ask you to cull 5 birds. They’ll take the carcases and store their organs so that these can be tested for the presence of antibiotics if the optional test is negative.

You can only get restrictions lifted, and sell eggs from the flock as Class A eggs if the samples test negative for a regulated salmonella.

Official samples from other flocks on your premises

Once APHA or DAERA identifies at least one flock as positive for Salmonella Enteritidis or Typhimurium, their officials will:

*take samples from every other flock on your premises - free of charge

*take samples when a new flock is placed in the house where the last positive test result occurred - free of charge for young birds not yet in production, routine official sample charges apply for adult laying birds

*visit your premises to give advice on salmonella control

If the positive result is from a rearing flock, your vet will decide how best to manage the risk of transferring infection to a laying site. There will also be restrictions on the flock.

Food chain information (FCI) documents for abattoirs

When you send your flock to an abattoir for slaughter for human consumption you must fill in a FCI declaration document.

In the FCI document, you must declare information such as:

* the most recent NCP test result

* the date the sample was collected

* other diagnoses

* the use of medicines

If it was a negative NCP result, you must also declare any previous positive salmonella test results with the sample collection dates. This includes results from tests not taken as part of the NCP programme.

You do not need to provide FCI if you cull the birds on the farm and:

*dispose of them as animal by-products

*sell your birds direct to customers

If selling your birds to customers, you should register this with your local authority. If your flock tests positive for salmonella, they will advise you on what you need to do to protect the health of your customers.

You’ll need a slaughterer’s licence or employ a licenced slaughterer to slaughter birds on your farm.

Get advice

If you need more advice, contact APHA or DAERA.

You can also read the code of practice for prevention of salmonella in chicken flocks.
