Guidance

Farming rules for water to prevent pollution from manure and fertiliser[a][b][c]

What you must do to manage organic manure, manufactured fertiliser and soil to prevent water pollution from runoff, erosion and leaching.

Published 23 May 2022

From:

Department for Environment, Food & Rural Affairs and Environment Agency

Applies to England

Plan before you apply organic manure or manufactured fertiliser

Legal requirements

Plan your application of organic manures and manufactured fertilisers to cultivated land.

You must:

  • avoid causing significant risk [d][e][f]of pollution
  • test cultivated agricultural soil for phosphorous, potassium, magnesium, pH and nitrogen levels at least every 5 years — you can use the soil nitrogen supply calculator to work out nitrogen levels instead of a soil test
  • use the results of these tests when you plan your applications to make sure you meet soil and crop nutrient needs

Terms explained

Organic manures are made from one or more animal, plant or human sources. They include farm yard manure that has been stored but not livestock droppings deposited directly onto land. They may be generated on the farm or come from off-farm sources.

Manufactured fertilisers are inorganic and bagged fertilisers containing nitrogen.

Applying includes:

  • spreading on the surface of the land
  • injecting into the soil
  • mixing manure or fertiliser with the surface layers of soil

Cultivated agricultural land is both or one of the following:

  • land you’ve ploughed, sowed or harvested at least once in the last year
  • land where you’ve applied organic manure or fertiliser at least once in the last 3 years

Best practice to plan your use of organic manure and fertiliser

Have a nutrient management plan. What you include in the plan is up to you. Use the plan to inform decisions about your use of organic manure and manufactured fertiliser to give your crops the nutrients they need.

Best practice to show you’ve considered the risk of pollution

Create a farm map that identifies areas that could be at risk from runoff or soil erosion.

Factors that could cause significant risk include:

  • angle of slopes, in particular if the angle is greater than 12 degrees
  • amount of ground cover
  • distance to inland freshwaters, coastal waters, wetlands, springs, wells or boreholes
  • soil type and condition
  • presence and condition of land drains

Avoid loss of nutrients through runoff and incorporate applied organic manure and manufactured fertiliser into the soil within 12 hours or as soon as possible if you can. This does not apply to grassland.

Best practise to work out soil nutrient and crop needs

You should:

  • plan what nutrients you are going to spread on the land
  • apply nutrients only in quantities that do not exceed the crop or soil need
  • use the results of soil sampling and analysis or a soil nitrogen supply assessment to work out how much to apply

When deciding what nutrients your crops need, you should use one of these:

  • a nutrient management guide, such as RB209 from the Agriculture and Horticulture Development Board
  • farm software such as PLANET or MANNER-NPK
  • nutrient management tools, such as those provided by Tried and Tested including a soil nitrogen supply calculator
  • a suitably qualified professional, such as an agronomist or Fertiliser Advisers Certification and Training Scheme (FACTS) adviser

You can identify the nutrient content of organic manure using nutrient management tools or manure testing, such as laboratory analysis or near infra-red sensors for liquid manures.

Other related legislation

If your farm is in a Nitrate vulnerable zone there are additional rules to follow.[g]

Storing organic manure

Rules you must follow when deciding where to store manure on your farm.

Legal requirements

You must not store organic manure:

  • within 10m of inland freshwaters or coastal waters
  • where risk factors like soil condition, nearby waterways or drains mean there’s a significant risk of pollution
  • within 50m of a spring, well or borehole

Best practice when you chose a site for manure storage

Before you make a manure store, check if there is a significant risk of pollution from the location.

There are significant risks of runoff and soil erosion from factors like the:

  • angle of slopes, in particular if the angle is greater than 12 degrees
  • amount of ground cover
  • soil type and condition
  • presence and condition of land drains

Check the area around stored organic manures and identify and block any potential pathways where runoff could flow into water.

On a farm map or plan, record the locations of:

  • waterways, field drains, springs, wells and boreholes
  • manure stores

Applying organic manure

Rules you must follow to reduce the risk of water pollution when applying, injecting or mixing manure with surface layers of soil.

Legal requirements

When you apply organic manure, you must:

  • have a plan for your application
  • take into account the weather conditions and forecasts at the time
  • take reasonable precautions to avoid significant risk of pollution

You must not apply organic manures if:

  • soil is waterlogged, flooded or snow covered
  • soil has been frozen for more than 12 hours in the previous 24 hours
  • there is significant risk of causing pollution

You must not apply organic manures within:

  • 10m of any inland freshwaters or coastal waters
  • 50m of a spring, well or borehole

Exceptions when you can apply within 10m

You can apply organic manures up to 6m from inland fresh or coastal waters if you use precision equipment.

These include:

  • a trailing hose or shoe band spreader
  • a shallow injector, no deeper than 10cm
  • a dribble bar applicator

You can apply livestock manure (not slurry or poultry) within 10m of inland freshwaters and coastal waters if you manage land for breeding wader birds or species-rich semi-natural grassland.

You can only do this if:

  • the land is in an Environmental or Countryside Stewardship scheme or it’s a site of special scientific interest (SSSI)
  • you do not apply manure onto the surface of water
  • you only apply the manure from 1 June to 31 October
  • you apply no more than 12.5 tonnes per hectare per year

Best practice when you apply manure

Before spreading manure, take reasonable precautions like check:

  • spreading equipment for leaks
  • the organic manure content and moisture levels of the soil

Try to incorporate organic manure into the soil within 12 hours or as soon as possible. This does not apply to grassland.

Record:

  • what you’ve spread and where
  • when you apply manure
  • the weather conditions at the time
  • any exceptions where you’ve used precision equipment or where on your land you’ve applied manures for environmental benefit

Other related regulations

Complying with landspreading permits details the types of materials that you can spread on your land.

Applying manufactured fertiliser

Rules you must follow to reduce the risk of pollution when you apply manufactured fertiliser.

Legal requirements

When you apply manufactured fertiliser, you must:

  • have a plan for your application
  • take into account the weather conditions and forecasts at the time
  • take reasonable precautions to avoid significant risk of pollution

You must not apply manufactured fertiliser:

  • if soil is waterlogged, flooded, or snow covered
  • if soil has been frozen for more than 12 hours in the previous 24 hours
  • if there is significant risk of causing pollution
  • within 2 metres of inland freshwaters, coastal waters, a spring, well or borehole

Best practice when you apply fertiliser

Before you apply fertiliser, take reasonable precautions to avoid pollution like check:

  • spreading equipment for leaks and correct calibration
  • the organic manure content in and moisture levels of the soil

Try to incorporate fertiliser into the soil within 12 hours or as soon as possible. This does not apply to grassland.

Record:

  • what you’re putting on - you can keep a product label or invoice

  • where you apply the fertiliser

  • date you put it on
  • weather conditions at the time

Other related regulations

  • Nitrate vulnerable zones

Take reasonable precautions to prevent diffuse pollution

Legal requirements

[h] ##

You must take reasonable precautions to prevent agricultural diffuse pollution from your land management and cultivation practices.

These types of practices include:

  • creating farm tracks or gateways
  • establishing seedbeds, polytunnels or tramlines
  • cleaning out ditches
  • installing drainage or irrigation
  • irrigating crops or spraying them with pesticides, herbicides or fungicides

Agricultural diffuse pollution is the transport of agricultural pollutants into inland or coastal waters. It can be transported by soil erosion or leaching and the pollutants may be harmful to human health or ecosystems.

Soil erosion is soil loss caused by soil runoff or degradation over a single area of agricultural land.

Best practice to avoid diffuse pollution from farms

Reasonable precautions include:

  • only do activities when conditions are suitable and in such a way that does not risk causing significant pollution
  • plant crops in early autumn and in dry conditions
  • undersow or sow a cover crop to stabilise soil after harvest - a cover crop is any crop with leaf cover that stops rain falling directly onto the soil
  • establish seedbeds, polytunnels or tramlines across the slope or with headland rows or beds across the base of sloping land
  • break up compacted soil
  • locate farm tracks or gateways where they pose as little risk of soil erosion into water as possible

Measures to reduce pollution risk if soil erosion does occur include establishing:

  • grass buffer strips in valleys, along contours, slopes, field edges and gateways
  • drainage ditches and sediment traps

On your farm map record:

  • what you’ve done to help reduce the risk of soil loss or water pollution from your activities
  • where these measures are

Manage livestock and feeders to prevent soil erosion

Rules to prevent livestock from causing diffuse pollution and soil erosion on your farm.

Legal requirements

You must not place livestock feeders:

  • within 10m of inland freshwaters or coastal waters
  • within 50m of a spring, well or borehole
  • where risk factors like slope, soil conditions or nearby water sources or land drains mean there’s a significant risk of pollution

You must prevent livestock from eroding soil by trampling it (poaching) within 5m of inland freshwater or coastal water.

You must take reasonable precautions to prevent diffuse pollution as a result of livestock activities.

Best practice when you choose locations for livestock feeders Record

  • where you place livestock feeders on a farm map or plan.

Best practice to prevent livestock causing soil loss

Move livestock regularly to prevent soil loss and pollution from poaching.

Take reasonable precautions to prevent erosion and runoff over a single area of one or more hectares as a result of livestock. You should:

  • monitor the impact of livestock on your fields
  • move livestock regularly or if there’s evidence of soil loss or pollution from poaching next to inland or coastal waters
  • avoid poaching over a single stretch of agricultural land of at least 2 metres wide and 20 metres long
  • erect fencing around inland freshwaters or coastal waters
  • winter livestock on well-drained, level fields

Record any precautions you’ve taken to reduce the risk of compacting soil.
