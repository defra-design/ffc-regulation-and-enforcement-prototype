Fertilisers that contain nitrogen

Nitrogen fertilisers can be manufactured (inorganic, bagged) fertilisers that you buy, other materials containing nitrogen (like waste soil) or organic manures that you produce or bring on to your holding.

Organic manures come from animals, plants or humans. They include:

  • slurry
  • poultry manures
  • solid manures (such as farmyard manure, sludge cake or compost)
  • sewage sludge (also called biosolids)
  • other liquid manures (such as abattoir waste or anaerobic digestate)

There’s a limit on the average amount of manufactured fertiliser and crop-available nitrogen from organic manure that you can apply to most crops each year - this is known as the N-max limit.

When you can apply more nitrogen

You can apply more nitrogen to some crops if your expected yield is higher than the ‘standard crop yield’ shown in the table.

You can use an additional 80kg of nitrogen a hectare if you’ve used straw or paper sludge on the previous or current crop.

Apply more nitrogen to wheat and barley

For wheat and barley, you can use an additional 20kg of nitrogen a hectare for every tonne that the expected yield exceeds the standard yield.

On milling wheat varieties, you can use an additional 40kg of nitrogen a hectare.

On autumn and winter-sown wheat and winter barley you can use an additional 20kg of nitrogen a hectare on fields with a shallow soil type (except on shallow soils over sandstone).

Apply more nitrogen to winter oilseed rape

On winter oilseed rape you can use up to 250kg of nitrogen a hectare. This includes a maximum autumn application of 30kg of manufactured nitrogen fertiliser a hectare.

If you use 30kg of nitrogen a hectare in the autumn, you can only use up to 220kg of nitrogen a hectare in the spring. You can increase this by an additional 30kg of nitrogen a hectare for every half tonne that the expected yield exceeds the standard yield.

Apply more nitrogen to grass

On grass that’s cut at least 3 times in a year, you can use an additional 40kg of nitrogen a hectare.

You can use nitrogen up to the level recommended in writing by a FACTS-qualified adviser if you’re growing grass to achieve a protein content of at least 16% in the dried product.

If the land is irrigated, you must not use more than 700kg of nitrogen a hectare. If the land isn’t irrigated, you must not use more than 500kg of nitrogen a hectare.

In subsequent years, you must sample these fields between 1 September and 31 October for soil mineral nitrogen levels and give your FACTS-qualified adviser the results.
