Legal requirements

You must test for salmonella if you keep 350 or more laying hens of the same species at any time in 12 months.

You must:

  • take samples from chicks on the day they arrive from the hatchery

  • test pullets before they move to the laying unit

  • test adult hens every 15 weeks starting when they are 22 to 26 weeks old

  • label the samples and send to an approved UK laboratory
  • not sell eggs as Class A if your flock tests positive

  • keep salmonella test results, hen movement and medicine records for at least 2 years

  • tell the Animal and Plant Health Agency (APHA) or the Department of Agriculture, Environment and Rural Affairs (DAERA) when you expect 250 or more hens to arrive on your holding

  • allow APHA to take official samples once a year (and pay for these)

Why you must test for salmonella

It is part of the national control programme (NCP) to reduce salmonella in poultry. You are breaking the law and may be prosecuted if you do not follow the rules.

Importing hatching eggs or chicks

If you import hatching eggs or day old chicks from Europe, check they have been tested for salmonella.

Before you test for salmonella

Register places where you keep flocks

You must register all premises where you keep flocks of 50 birds or more.

In Scotland you must also register your laying flocks with the Scottish Government Poultry Unit for egg marketing reasons.

In Northern Ireland you must register all poultry flocks on the Northern Ireland Poultry Register. This includes single backyard hens. The only exception is if it is a pet bird that lives in the ownerâ€™s house.

Ask your vet or laboratory for advice

They can help you:

  • find an an approved UK laboratory or find a DAERA approved laboratory if you are in Northern Ireland

  • create sampling plans

  • train staff to take the samples correctly

  • get the sampling kit you need

You can make use of other training schemes like Lion Training Passport.

Work out how many flocks you have to sample

A flock is a group of birds that shares the same air space, shed or range. You must take samples from all the flocks at your premises.

You need to decide how many flocks you keep, so you collect all the necessary samples. If your birds form one flock and have a positive sample, they will all be treated as belonging to a positive flock.

Deciding whether you have one or more flocks could affect how a positive result and official disease controls affect your business financially.

If you need help to work out how many flocks you have, ask:

  • APHA in England, Scotland and Wales
  • DAERA in Northern Ireland

When to take samples from laying hens

Avoid taking samples after giving antibiotics

Avoid taking samples while, or shortly after, giving your laying hens antimicrobials (antibiotics) that affect salmonella.

APHA or DAERA could consider your flock to be positive for salmonella if:

  • inspectors see from the flock medicine book that you have given antibiotics that affect salmonella within 14 days before NCP testing

  • the laboratory suspects that there may be disinfectant or antibiotics affecting your samples

To find out when you can sample after giving antibiotics, ask:

  • the vet who prescribed them

  • APHA in England, Scotland and Wales

  • DAERA in Northern Ireland

Collect samples from chicks on the day they arrive from the hatchery

You should test:

  • the liners (chick papers) from boxes used to deliver the chicks - one box for every 500 chicks from each delivery (up to 10 boxes)

  • any chicks that are dead on arrival (up to 60 from each hatchery)

Collect samples from pullets 2 weeks before you move them to the laying unit

You should test:

  • 2 pairs of boot swabs (fabric overshoes) for your floor-reared birds

  • 60 grams (g) of a composite faeces sample

Composite means the faeces are from many birds in the flock.

Collect samples from your adult egg-laying hens every 15 weeks during the laying period

If officials collect routine samples during the laying period, you do not need to test again for 15 weeks.

You must start to take samples from adult laying hens in the layer unit or house when they are 22 to 26 weeks old.

For barn or free-range flocks, you should test:

  • 2 pairs of boot swabs when not part of a multi-tier system

  • 1 pair of boot swabs and one or more hand-held faecal swabs when part of a multi-tier system

For cage flocks, you should test:

  • 2 composite faeces samples of 150g each for houses with scrapers or belt cleaners

  • one or more fabric swabs for houses without scrapers or belt cleaners

How to take boot swab, composite and fabric swab samples

Prepare to take samples

Gather all the equipment you are going to need before you go into the laying house, to prevent contamination before, during and after sampling. Take care to avoid cross-contamination if you keep other animals (especially other poultry, pigs or cattle).

Take boot swab samples

Take samples from the existing litter. Do not put new litter down in the 2 days before sampling.

You will need:

  • disposable plastic overboots - put these on only after you have walked through any disinfectant boot-dip
  • one or 2 pair(s) of boot swabs
  • tap water or bottled still water to moisten boot swabs - do not use sparkling water or water treated with antibacterial agents or acids

  • disposable plastic gloves - do not use hand sanitiser as it could affect your sample

  • leak-proof sealable bags or sample pots

  • packaging for sending your sealable bags or sample pots to the laboratory

To take samples from each flock

  1. Take one or 2 pair(s) of boot swabs for each flock, depending on your sampling method.

  2. Moisten the boot swabs (if not already moist) with the tap water or bottled still water.

  3. For 2 pairs of boot swabs, divide the house into 2 equal parts for sampling and use one pair of boot swabs in each part. For one pair of boot swabs, walk round the whole house.

  4. Take at least 100 steps with each pair of boot swabs, walking round the entire area to be sampled.

  5. Drag your feet on the floor as you are walking, to pick up as much material as you can.

  6. If the house is divided into several pens, spend more time in the larger pens and less in the smaller pens to make sure every pen is included in the sampling.

  7. When you have finished sampling, take the boot swabs off and carefully turn them inside out so that the faeces you have collected stay on them.

  8. Put the boot swabs in a sample pot or sealable bag.

  9. Label each container carefully, ready to send to the laboratory.

Take composite faeces samples

You will need:

  • disposable plastic gloves - do not use hand sanitiser as it could affect your sample

  • inverted bags or spatula to collect the samples

  • leak-proof sealable bags or sample pots for faeces or swabs

  • packaging for sending your sealable bags or sample pots to the laboratory

Take composite faeces samples from dropping belts or scrapers

To get a representative and fresh sample you should:

  • run belts or scrapers just before you take the samples

  • collect faeces from scrapers at the discharge ends of the dropping belts on every row and tier of the cages

Take composite faeces samples without dropping belts or scrapers

If there are no belts, you must collect samples from cages or across the area of the dropping pit. Make sure the material you collect is representative of the whole house.

For sampling young birds:

  1. Collect at least 60 individual samples of faeces that weigh at least 1 gram (g).

  2. Send the whole sample (at least 60g of faeces) in a pot or sealable bag to the laboratory.

For sampling adult birds:

  1. Collect 2 samples of at least 150g faeces each (150g is about the amount that would fit in a coffee mug).

  2. Collect samples of mixed fresh faeces from at least 60 different places beneath the cages in the dropping pits.

  3. Send the samples in a pot or sealable bag to the laboratory.

Take fabric swab samples

You will need:

  • one or more fabric swabs with a total surface area of at least 900 square centimeters (sq cms)

  • tap water or bottled still water for moistening fabric swabs which are not already pre-moistened - do not use sparkling water or water treated with antibacterial agents or acids

  • disposable plastic gloves - do not use hand sanitiser as it could affect your sample

  • leak-proof sealable bags or sample pots

  • packaging for sending your sealable sample bags or sample pots to the laboratory

To take the fabric swab samples:

  1. Put on new plastic gloves - make sure you do not contaminate them with disinfectant.

  2. Open out the fabric swabs.

  3. If not already moistened, moisten the fabric swabs with the tap water or bottled still water.

  4. Run belts or scrapers just before you take the samples.

  5. Swab as large an area as possible at the discharge end of all accessible belts, scrapers and belt cleaners.

  6. Make sure both sides of the swab are completely covered in faeces.

  7. Place the swabs into sealable bags or sample pots to send to the laboratory.

What to do with the samples

Label samples

On each label, you must include:

  • the unique identifier of the specific flock

  • flock house name or number

  • month and year you moved the flock into that house

On the label, or the laboratoryâ€™s submission form or system, provide the:

  • type(s) of sample taken

  • date you took the sample

  • registration number, County Parish Holding (CPH) number, or DAERA flock number

  • name and address of the flockâ€™s premises

  • age of the flock

  • contact details of the person sending the sample

If you use a fabric bootswab as a hand swab, you must make sure that this is accurately labelled, so that the laboratory can identify the sample type in its report.

Send samples to a UK approved laboratory

You must send your samples on the day you collect them. If you do not send them within 24 hours you must refrigerate them at 2 to 8Â°C. Samples must be sent within 48 hours. You must not freeze samples.

Check that the laboratory can start testing your samples within 4 days of sampling.

You will need to send more samples if your samples are delayed, or the laboratory cannot meet the testing deadline.

The laboratory will send your results to:

  • the person who sent the sample or the registered owner of the sampled flock

  • APHA or DAERA - if the sample tests positive for salmonella (this may be referred to as a Zoonoses Order or a ZO2 notification by some laboratories)

If your samples test positive for regulated strains of salmonella

APHA or DAERA will assess the positive tests for regulated strains of salmonella (these are non-vaccine strain Salmonella enteritidis or Salmonella typhimurium, including monophasic Salmonella typhimurium).

You must not sell the eggs as Class A from a flock that has tested positive. You will be legally restricted from selling these eggs.

Egg marketing inspectors will visit your premises to check that you are complying with the restrictions.

If you wish to sell the eggs as Class B, you can if you mark them with either:

  • the official Class B mark (a circle with the letter B inside)
  • a spray of food dye to leave a visible coloured mark at 5 millimetres in diameter

Additional testing for salmonella after identifying a regulated strain

Contact APHA or DAERA if you have evidence showing contamination of the original sample.

In exceptional cases, APHA or DAERA may agree to additional tests to rule out a false positive result. They will only permit the additional tests when there is:

  • no evidence that a flock is linked to a human outbreak
  • strong evidence that the original result may be false

These tests must be carried out within 4 weeks of the APHA or DAERA laboratory reporting the positive sample and at your own expense.

APHA or DAERA will arrange for an inspector to supervise the collection, choosing one from the following sample options:

  • 300 hen carcasses
  • 4,000 eggs
  • 2 dust samples and 5 boot swab samples from barn and free-range birds
  • 2 dust samples and 5 pooled faeces samples from caged birds

Depending on the test results, APHA or DAERA will decide if and when to lift the restrictions on your flock.

Official samples from other flocks on your premises

Once APHA or DAERA identifies at least one flock as positive, their officials will take samples from every other flock on your premises.

Officials will also visit and sample when a new flock is placed in the house where the last flock had a positive test result. They will take this official control sample when the birds are between 22 and 26 weeks old.

A government vet will also visit your premises to give advice on salmonella control. In Northern Ireland contact DAERA or your private vet for advice.

You will not have to pay for:

  • official sampling of other flocks on your premises
  • testing the new flock following its move to the house which officials identified as positive

Positive results from a rearing flock

If a sample from your rearing flock tests positive for a regulated salmonella, officials may visit your farm to confirm the result and offer advice.

Get advice on controlling other salmonella infections

If your flock tests positive for other types of salmonella, you can get advice on how to control the infection from:

  • your vet
  • APHA if you live in England, Scotland or Wales
  • DAERA if you live in Northern Ireland

Keep salmonella test, movement and medicine records for at least 2 years

Keep salmonella test records

For each sample you must record the:

  • date you took the sample

  • flock identification (this is the flockâ€™s house name or number, and the month and year the flock moved into the house)

  • type(s) of sample (such as boot swabs or pooled faeces)

  • age of the flock sampled (there may be more than one age if the sample is from a multi-age house)

  • laboratory that tested the sample

  • test result

Keep movement records

You need to record any movement of birds or eggs (except eggs for human consumption) to or from your premises. For each movement record the:

  • date (month and year) the flock moved on to or off your premises

  • number of birds or eggs

  • age of the birds

  • house name or number

  • address that the birds or eggs moved from (including the building name or number)

  • address that the birds or eggs moved to (including the building name or number)

Keep medication records

You must record the:

  • medicines used

  • dose rate

  • start and end date of treatment

  • route of administration

  • end date of the withdrawal period

Tell APHA or DAERA when a laying flock of 250 birds arrives

You must tell APHA or DAERA 2 weeks before the date you expect every laying flock of at least 250 birds to arrive at your holding.

If you farm in England, Scotland and Wales, contact APHA Customer Service Centre: One Health Worcester.

If you farm in Northern Ireland, contact DAERA.

You need to include the:

  • name, address and telephone number of the holding
  • name, address and telephone number of the occupier and the person who owns each breeding flock on the holding
  • numbers of flocks on the holding

For each flock, include the:

  • identification of the chicken laying flock
  • the number of poultry

Official sampling at your premises

If you keep more than 1,000 birds on your premises, officials will take a routine official sample once a year.

In England, Scotland and Wales, routine official samples may be taken by an:

  • APHA official
  • authorised vet
  • Independent Control Body (ICB) auditor (if your flock is in the Lion Code scheme)

In Northern Ireland, an official from DAERA will take routine official samples.

You will be charged for these visits. An official sample can replace an operator sample.

Find out about official sampling fees for England, Scotland and Wales.

Contact DAERA for details of fees in Northern Ireland.

Include test results in food chain information (FCI) documents for abattoirs

When you send your flock to an abattoir for slaughter for human consumption you must fill in a FCI declaration document.

In the FCI document, you must declare information such as:

  • the most recent NCP test result

  • the date the sample was collected

  • other diagnoses

  • the use of medicines

If it was a negative NCP result, you must also declare any previous positive salmonella test results with the sample collection dates. This includes results from tests not taken as part of the NCP programme.

You do not need to provide FCI if you cull the birds on the farm and:

  • dispose of them as animal by-products

  • sell your birds direct to customers

If selling your birds to customers, you should register this with your local authority. If your flock tests positive for salmonella, they will advise you on what you need to do to protect the health of your customers.

You will need a slaughtererâ€™s licence or employ a licenced slaughterer to slaughter birds on your farm.

Get advice

If you need more advice, contact APHA or DAERA.

You can also read the code of practice for prevention of salmonella in chicken flocks.
