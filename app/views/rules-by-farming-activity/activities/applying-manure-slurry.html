Guidance

Applying manure or slurry to land

What you must do to plan for and apply manure or slurry. How to protect water and the environment from run off and leaching.

Contents

  2. Assess risks of causing pollution
  3. Page 3

You must take steps to prevent manure getting into watercourses – known as diffuse water pollution (pollution).

Organic manures are made from one or more animal, plant or human sources.

These rules apply to farming or horticultural practices that involve the use and storage of organic manure (manure).

Plan your use of manure or slurry

You must plan each application of slurry and organic manure on your land so that they do not give rise to a significant risk of pollution.

Applying includes:

  • spreading on the surface of the land
  • injecting into the soil
  • mixing manure with the surface layers of soil

For all farming and horticultural land, you must plan:

  • how much manure to use, so you do not use more than your soil and crop needs
  • and take into account fertilser applications
  • by assessing the pollution risks

You can use the Nutrient Management Guide RB209 on the Agriculture and Horticulture Development Board website to work out the nutrients your soil or crop needs.

Soil tests for cultivated agricultural land

If you’re appyling manure to cultivated agricultural land, you must also plan by using the results of soil test results.

Cultivated agricultural land is both or one of:

  • land you’ve ploughed, sowed or harvested at least once in the last year
  • land where you’ve applied organic manure or fertiliser at least once in the last 3 years

The results of soil tests must show the pH and levels of:

  • nitrogen (N) - you can use a soil nitrogen supply calculator instead of a soil test
  • phosphorus (P)
  • potassium (K)
  • magnesium (Mg)

Soil test results must be no more than 5 years old at the time of application.

When deciding what nutrients your crops need, you should use one of these:

  • a nutrient management guide, such as RB209 from the Agriculture and Horticulture Development Board
  • farm software such as PLANET or MANNER-NPK
  • nutrient management tools, such as those provided by Tried and Tested including a < a href="https://www.nutrientmanagement.org/what-we-do/calculators-and-estimators/">soil nitrogen supply calculatorx
  • a suitably qualified professional, such as an agronomist or Fertiliser Advisers Certification and Training Scheme (FACTS) adviser

You can identify the nutrient content of organic manure using nutrient management tools or manure testing, such as laboratory analysis or near infra-red sensors for liquid manures.

Planning can:

  • help you optimise the timing and number of applications
  • save you money as you use less manufactured fertiliser
  • reduce the risk of diffuse pollution and improve water quality in your area

Assess risks of causing pollution

You must take reasonable precautions to reduce the risk of pollution when you apply manure.

Examples include:

  • taking into account the weather conditions and forecasts at the time you want to apply manure on your land
  • checking your spreading equipment is calibrated and does not leak
  • checking the presence and condition of land drains
  • working manure into the soil as soon as possible after applying it - this does not apply to grassland
  • checking the organic matter content and moisture level in your soil – you can use the Nutrient Management Guide RB209 on the Agriculture and Horticulture Development Board website or other resources to help you do this

Recommended actions to show you’ve planned applications of manure

To show that you have planned for crop and soil need:

  • produce a written nutrient management plan and update it every year
  • keep field records showing crop type and what organic and manufactured fertilisers you’ve applied
  • use a recognised planning tool or a FACTS qualified advisor

Produce a risk map of the farm showing areas where manure can and can’t be spread. You may find it useful to include where you have:

  • sloping land
  • watercourses and land drains
  • different soil types

Calculate your Livestock Manure Nitrogen Farm limit (must be less than 170kgN/ha).

Check the current ground conditions. Be prepared to postpone if the conditions are not right on the day.

Applying manure

You must not apply manure:

  • on soil that is waterlogged, flooded or snow covered
  • when the soil has been frozen for more than 12 hours in the previous 24 hours
  • within 10m of any inland freshwaters or coastal waters
  • within 50m of a spring, well or borehole
  • if there is no crop or soil need – in most cases there is no crop or soil need in autumn and winter
  • where the risk factors mean there’s a significant risk of pollution

6m exception for precision equipment

You can apply manure no closer than 6m from inland freshwaters or coastal waters if you use:

  • a trailing hose or shoe band spreader
  • a shallow injector (no deeper than 10cm)
  • a dribble bar applicator

Exception for environmental benefit

If you manage land for breeding wader birds or as a species-rich semi-natural grassland, you can apply livestock manure (not slurry or poultry) within 10m of inland freshwaters and coastal waters. You can only do this if:

  • the land is in an Environmental or Countryside Stewardship scheme or it’s a site of special scientific interest (SSSI)
  • you do not apply manure onto the surface of water
  • you only apply the manure from 1 June to 31 October
  • you apply no more than 12.5 tonnes per hectare per year

Keep records

Record:

  • where you’ve applied manure
  • risk factors in that area
  • what you’ve done to reduce the risk
  • when you applied the manure
  • the weather conditions at the time
  • any exceptions where you’ve used precision equipment or where on your land you’ve applied manures for environmental benefit

How enforcement will work

If the Environment Agency finds you’re breaching the rules, it will help you by:

  • identifying the changes you need to make
  • agreeing a timescale with you to make changes

To check you’ve made changes, the Environment Agency may:

  • give you a follow-up visit
  • ask for evidence, such as photographic evidence of a change

If there’s already pollution or a high risk of pollution, the Environment Agency may take enforcement action. This may include prosecution.

Source legislation

These rules are from The Reduction and Prevention of Agricultural Diffuse Pollution (England) Regulations 2018.
